Summary

1.1 What was happening in the original schedule (issues that forced change)
The original schedule had multiple weeks where key people were booked well beyond the 40-hour weekly capacity. This directly hurts the Fitness Score because Conflict is the biggest driver (50% of the score). In plain terms: too much work was stacked onto the same individuals in the same weeks, creating overtime risk and a high chance of missed deadlines or burnout.

At a portfolio level, work was also more “peaky” (big spikes in certain weeks), which typically creates fire-drills and last-minute reshuffling. That pattern also tends to worsen multitasking and overload.

1.2 What is happening now (updated schedule, high-level)
The updated schedule keeps the total project workload the same, but redistributes it by:
Adding more person-project-role assignments (more rows), meaning work is spread across more people while staying within role constraints.
Rebalancing the weekly distribution of hours across the calendar so workload is slightly less spiky overall.
Keeping projects largely in their original timing bands (start/end windows are respected; changes are mainly reallocations and spreading hours rather than wholesale project moves).

Net effect: the plan is more resilient and slightly less overloaded, but there are still several serious over-capacity weeks that remain unresolved.

1.3 Likely optimization intent (inferred from observed changes)
Based on the differences, the “greedy” optimizer appears to have prioritized:
Reducing overload first (Conflict Score): it made changes that slightly reduced the number of over-capacity person-weeks.
Spreading work across more qualified people (Focus vs. Continuity tradeoff): it introduced additional staffing lines on several projects, which usually indicates it tried to offload work from overloaded individuals to others with available capacity in the same role.
Smoothing workload across weeks: overall weekly total-hours volatility decreased, which generally helps reduce staffing crunches even if it doesn’t fully eliminate individual overloads.

It looks like the optimizer accepted some additional team mixing (potentially lowering Continuity a bit) in exchange for lowering the risk of over-capacity weeks (which matters more due to Conflict’s 50% weight).

1.4 Recommendations (where >40 hour weeks cannot be resolved)
Even after optimization, there are still 11 person-week conflicts (weeks where someone exceeds 40 hours). Some of these are extremely high, indicating the schedule is not realistically executable without one or more of the following business actions:

Add short-term capacity in the affected weeks (contractors/temp staff) for the overloaded roles.
Re-negotiate deadlines or re-phase scope for the projects driving the spikes around weeks W20–W23.
Increase role-based cross-coverage (if allowed in your business) by enabling more people to perform that role, since the biggest remaining overloads suggest not enough qualified capacity is available in that window.

The remaining conflicts are concentrated heavily around W20–W23 and are dominated by a few individuals:
Person_02: up to 130h in W22 (90h over capacity)
Person_01: up to 125h in W21 (85h over capacity)
Person_03: up to 125h in W21 (85h over capacity)

That magnitude strongly suggests a structural capacity shortfall in that period, not just a minor balancing issue.
